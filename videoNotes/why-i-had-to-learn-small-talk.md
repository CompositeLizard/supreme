## What the Hell is Small Talk?
Ok, let's start with what the hell is small talk?

So in Moldova, small talk is rare. We go straight to the point, you ask a question, you give the answer according to the question. The answer has to be honest, has to be accurate, and has to be actual, has to be up to date to the reality, to the current time. And for us, it's not a problem to start with negatives and tell you that my cat is sick or my back hurts or I have so many things to do and I'm so stressed out. That's part of our small talk.

But in English-speaking countries, small talk is like social glue. You show your friendliness, your approachability every time you meet someone new. So small talk is a cultural thing. You don't have to love it, but you need it if you want to connect with people outside your country and get that networking going.

## Why Does Small Talk Feel Awkward?
So why does small talk feel awkward? It's because we overthink it. What do I say? How do I say it? Am I boring? Does that person want to know this? Do they want to hear me out? Are they interested? So many different questions in the moment.

But here's the truth. Small talk is not about you saying what you need to say. It's about making the other person comfortable and you showing interest in them.

## Seizing the Opportunity
So imagine you're at a networking event, at a conference or something, and the person right next to you could be your potential new client or your potential new partner or colleague. If you don't say anything, you miss that chance. And trust me, the other person thinks the same way. They're probably also nervous to say something. They're probably also overthinking what they want to say or are about to say. So one of you has to take the first step. So why not be you?

Consider small talk like a little bridge over to bigger conversations, more serious conversations. For example, if you're in an elevator, you have a very short amount of time to create this connection with someone, right? So a simple "Hi, how are you?" or "Hi, how's your day?" is enough.

If you are standing in line at a coffee shop, right? You can ask things like, "Have you been here before? Are their pastries any good? Do you like this coffee? Can you recommend anything good here?" Literally anything for those couple of minutes that you're in line together so the silence isn't awkward. And you never know, that could literally be your neighbor and you would have never known.

So here's a little secret. Small talk is actually not fake. Small talk is an empathy exercise. You are saying to the other person indirectly, "I see you, I care enough to ask you something. I care enough to start a conversation with you."

## How to Actually Get Good at Small Talk

**Rule number one: Don't try to be interesting. Try to be interested.**
People love talking about themselves, so you have to let them. You have to be there to listen more than to talk. You can ask questions, you can notice little details, maybe about their outfit, you can compliment them, and you always have to finish your sentence with another question because you're passing the mic to the other person.

And if you're a little bit shy or you're a little bit introverted, that's totally fine. What you need to do is just to have a few questions ready to go whenever you need them. For example:
*   How is your day going?
*   What brings you here?
*   How do you know the host?
*   What's your role in this company?

Literally anything connected to the area, the circumstance, the environments that you're in. (Oh my God, there's a squirrel.) So if you want the conversations to keep flowing naturally, you just have to finish your sentence with another question. And then if they know that, they will finish their sentence with another question.

**Questions are the magic trick.**
It just takes the pressure off of you and lets them talk. And because that person likes talking about them, you just ask them another question, and then another question, and then another question. This way you find out a lot more about that person and you don't have to do a lot of work.

**Match the Energy (Mirroring)**
Another point here is to match the other person's energy. This is called the mirroring. You ask a question, if that person is showing signs that they want to answer, they want to talk to you, you continue talking. If that person is very short and kind of like maybe turns away a little bit and is not asking you a question back, you match that energy. You don't really continue, you don't really push that conversation too far, but at least you've tried and now you know.

## The Hiring Perspective: Why Soft Skills Win
I'm going to give you a very quick example from when I was a manager at this restaurant in Canada. Because I was a manager, I was also part of the recruiting team, so I was doing interviews for candidates for certain jobs. And when these candidates would come in, the first thing that I would look at is how they made me feel and how they would connect with me literally from the first second.

And because it would have been extremely awkward for us to sit and for me to immediately start, "So tell me about your previous job," right? That feels a little too straight to the point. So we always had some sort of interaction, like a couple seconds, couple questions here and there. And be like, "Did you find us easy? How was your day? How was the commute?" Like anything really.

And then based on my question, I would see how the person would react. Are they sharp enough to give me the right answer? Are they using the correct facial expressions? Are they funny? Are they cracking a joke? Are they responding well to my question? Or they just get super terrified to say anything because they just rehearsed the interview questions and that's all.

So the fact that I would feel a certain awkwardness if they weren't able to reply to my small talk question would tell me how they would behave in any unexpected situation at work. You have to present yourself as friendly and welcoming and approachable to your colleagues, to the guests, to your bosses, to everybody around you. And if that doesn't really work, then your job is going to be extremely, extremely difficult. And then my job will be extremely difficult following up on the other person's job.

And why is it difficult? So if I were to choose between somebody who has a smile on their face, are comfortable, are responding well to my small talk, are pleasant, they're making me feel relaxed, they're approachable, they're friendly, they're sociable, and another person who has a lot of experience but is not able to make me feel like that, I would hire the person with zero experience because you can teach technical skills, you can teach the job, but you cannot teach personal skills and I don't have the time to teach them small talk skills too.
